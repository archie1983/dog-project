Step 1: Detect Humans

The submission returns the percentage of the first 100 images in the dog and human face datasets with a detected human face.

Good. The percentage of the first 100 images in human_filenames and in dog_filenames were detected and correctly returned. Ideally, we would like to have 100% of human images with a detected face and 0% of dog images with a detected face but the algorithm falls short as shown in the answers with 100% of human and 11% of dog images.

The submission opines whether Haar cascades for face detection are an appropriate technique for human detection.

Thank you for giving an opinion, there is no right or wrong answer here but it is good to get the opinion of everyone.
I think it is a reasonable expectation to the user, but finally this depends on what we want to build. We may implement an algorithm that detects other specificity of a human, e.g. eyes, eyebrows, mouth, nose, hands, and define what is necessary for a full face picture. The algorithm can be further optimized by providing more training data with human faces in different angles and fine tuning the model.

Suggestion

You could experiment too with batch normalization in the network. This works by normalizing the weighted inputs in the network. Without it, only the inputs at the beginning of the network are normalized. More information about this found on Quora can be seen here why does batch normaization help.

The submission specifies the number of epochs used to train the algorithm.

Extra Tips

To get everything out of your network and data, the number of epochs should be chosen such that the validation accuracy is no longer increasing. This can be done manually or you can use the early stopping callback function of Keras found on this link.

The trained model attains at least 1% accuracy on the test set.

To improve on your accuracy, you can try to augment the data. See this keras blog on Building powerful image classification models using very little data

Even though the architecture chosen worked out as expected, it would be good to try out or make other attempts with different architectures and find out the reason they aren't working the way they are supposed to be or why they work better. Extra Tips

Check out this paper on Speed/accuracy trade-offs for modern convolutional object detectors and the blog post ImageNet: VGGNet, ResNet, Inception, and Xception with Keras.

Suggestion

Rather than using rmsprop optimizer you might want to try out the adam optimizer. It is a better choice as it's configuration is way easier and it always tends to give superior results. You can read more about it from this site Gentle Introduction to the Adam Optimization Algorithm for Deep Learning
